# Chapter 7 — Central Bank Language and Macro Signals

This chapter tackles the most market-moving scheduled text events in finance — central bank communications. It covers FOMC statement analysis, the evolution of forward guidance, hawkish/dovish language models, and cross-central bank comparative analysis. The chapter connects language parsing to macro nowcasting.

## FOMC Statements, Minutes, and Press Conferences

Map the structure, release timing, and information content hierarchy of Federal Reserve communications. Statements are released immediately after meetings, minutes follow with a three-week lag, and press conferences provide real-time Q&A — each layer adding nuance and occasionally surprises.

## The Evolution of Fed Communication

Trace the shift from opacity to forward guidance and explain how communication policy changes affect NLP approaches. The Fed's language regime has shifted dramatically over decades, meaning models trained on recent text may fail on historical analysis and vice versa.

## Hawkish/Dovish Language Models

Build a continuous policy tone index from central bank text, covering model design, calibration, and validation. The hawk-dove spectrum is the most widely used dimension for central bank sentiment, but reducing complex policy communication to a single axis requires careful design choices.

## Cross-Central Bank Language Analysis

Compare communication styles and NLP approaches across ECB, BoJ, and BoE, identifying transferable and institution-specific patterns. Each central bank has a distinct communication culture, and models that work well for Fed text may require significant adaptation for other institutions.

## Nowcasting Macro Indicators from Language

Use central bank text to nowcast economic variables like inflation expectations and growth outlook before official data releases. Central bankers have access to proprietary data and analysis, and their language choices — however careful — reveal information about their economic outlook.

## The "Fed Whisperer" Problem

Address the challenge of markets reading between the lines — when the signal is in what is *not* said as much as what is. Absence of expected language, subtle word substitutions, and structural changes in communication all carry meaning that pure bag-of-words approaches miss.
